Heritage

Ancient Cities

Image source: Unsplash

Sri Lanka is home to several ancient cities that showcase the country's rich cultural heritage. These cities were once bustling centers of trade, religion, and politics, and they are now popular tourist destinations. Some of the most famous ancient cities in Sri Lanka include Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa, and Sigiriya. Anuradhapura was the first capital of Sri Lanka and is home to several impressive Buddhist temples and monuments. Polonnaruwa, the second capital of Sri Lanka, features well-preserved ruins of ancient palaces, temples, and other structures. Sigiriya, also known as the Lion Rock, is a UNESCO World Heritage site that boasts a towering rock fortress and beautiful frescoes. A visit to these ancient cities provides a glimpse into Sri Lanka's fascinating past and a deeper understanding of the country's rich cultural heritage.

Cuisine

Image source: Unsplash

The cuisine of Sri Lanka draws influence from that of India, especially from Kerala, as well as colonists and foreign traders. Rice, which is usually consumed daily, can be found at any special occasion, while spicy curries are favourite dishes for lunch and dinner. A very popular alcoholic drink is toddy or arrack, both made from palm tree sap. Rice and curry refers to a range of Sri Lankan dishes. Sri Lankans also eat hoppers (Aappa, Aappam), which can be found anywhere in Sri Lanka.

Much of Sri Lanka’s cuisine consists of boiled or steamed rice served with spicy curry. Another well-known rice dish is kiribath, meaning milk rice. Curries in Sri Lanka are not just limited to meat or fish- based dishes; there are also vegetable and even fruit curries. A typical Sri Lankan meal consists of a “main curry” (fish, chicken, or mutton), as well as several other curries made with vegetable and lentils. Side-dishes include pickles, chutneys and “sambols” which can sometimes be fiery hot. The most famous of these is the coconut sambol, made of scraped coconut mixed with chilli peppers, dried Maldivian fish and lime juice. This is ground to a paste and eaten with rice, as it gives zest to the meal and is believed to increase appetite.

Many of Sri Lanka’s urban areas are host to American fast food corporations and many of the younger generation have started to take a liking to this new style of cuisine although it is rejected by many, particularly the more traditional elder members of the community.

Festivals

Image source: Unsplash

The Sinhala and Tamil New Year (“Aluth Avurudhu”; in Sinhala, “Puthiyathandu”; in Tamil) is a very large cultural event on the island. The festival falls in April (also known as the month of Bak) when the sun moves from the Meena Rashiya (House of Pisces) to the Mesha Rashiya (House of Aries).

Unusually, both the end of one year and the beginning of the next occur not at midnight but at separate times determined by astrologers with a period of some hours between (the “nona gath”; or neutral period) being a time where one is expected to refrain from all types of work and instead engage solely in relaxing religious activities and traditional games. During the New Year, festivities both children and adults will often don traditional outfits. But the clothes must be washed and very clean because it should be southam (pure).

Dance and Music

Image source: Unsplash

Dance and singing have long been a component of Sri Lankan tradition, dating back over 2,000 years. Sri Lankan traditional dances are renowned for their complex footwork and elaborate outfits. Traditional dances are classified into several kinds, each with its own distinct style and significance. The Kandyan dance, for example, is a fast-paced, energetic performance that developed in Sri Lanka's central hills and is usually done during religious and cultural festivals. Meanwhile, the southern coastal region's low-country dance is known for its graceful motions and is frequently done to show pleasure and happiness.

Tradtitional Craft

Image source: Unsplash

Traditional crafts have been an integral part of Sri Lanka's cultural heritage for centuries. These crafts are not only beautiful and intricate but also have significant cultural and historical value. Sri Lanka is renowned for its traditional crafts such as batik, pottery, and weaving, which have been passed down from generation to generation.

Batik is a technique of wax-resist dyeing applied to cloth that is widely practiced in Sri Lanka. The process involves applying melted wax to specific parts of the fabric and then dying it. This technique creates beautiful patterns and designs that are unique to Sri Lanka.

Pottery is another traditional craft in Sri Lanka that has been practiced for over 3000 years. The pottery produced in Sri Lanka is renowned for its unique designs and high-quality finish. Sri Lankan potters use traditional techniques and equipment to create pottery, which is both functional and decorative.

Weaving is yet another traditional craft in Sri Lanka that has been practiced for centuries. Weavers in Sri Lanka use traditional handlooms to produce a wide range of fabrics that are used for clothing, upholstery, and home decor. The fabrics produced in Sri Lanka are known for their vibrant colors and intricate designs.

The coastal town of Ambalangoda is known for its wooden masks, which depict demons, gods and animals. Raksha masks, which portray 24 demons including the cobra and fire devil, are worn at festivals, while Sanni masks are used in exorcisms and Kolam masks for comic dances. A typical story sees the god of craftsmen provide masks and lyrics so servants can satisfy a pregnant queen’s cravings for dance.

New Year Celebrations and Customs

Image source: Unsplash

Sri Lankan New Year, also known as "Aluth Avurudhu" in Sinhala, is one of the most important cultural celebrations in Sri Lanka. It usually falls on the 13th or 14th of April, marking the beginning of the traditional New Year in the country. This celebration is based on the ancient astrological belief of the sun's movement from the Meena Rashiya (House of Pisces) to the Mesha Rashiya (House of Aries) in the zodiac calendar.

The New Year celebration in Sri Lanka is a time for family reunions and festive activities. The customs and rituals associated with this celebration are unique to the country and have been passed down through generations. The celebration usually lasts for several days, with each day having its own set of customs and traditions.

The day before the New Year is called "Punya Kalaya" and it is believed to be a time of transition between the old and the new. It is a time for cleaning and preparing the house for the New Year. People also engage in religious activities and offer prayers at temples and other holy sites.

On the day of the New Year, the first ritual is to light the hearth and boil milk in a new clay pot. This is known as the "Ganu Denu" ceremony and is believed to bring prosperity and good luck for the New Year. After this, the family sits down together to enjoy a traditional breakfast consisting of sweetmeats, fruits, and other delicacies.

Religious Harmony in Sri Lanka

Image source: Unsplash

Sri Lanka is a diverse country with multiple religions practiced by its citizens. The main religions in Sri Lanka are Buddhism, Hinduism, Islam, and Christianity.

  • Buddhism is the largest religion in Sri Lanka, practiced by around 70% of the population. It was introduced to Sri Lanka by Emperor Ashoka of India in the 3rd century BCE. Sri Lanka's Buddhist traditions are a blend of Theravada Buddhism and ancient animistic beliefs. The country has a rich history of Buddhist art and architecture, with many ancient temples and monuments, such as the Temple of the Tooth in Kandy and the ancient city of Anuradhapura.
  • Hinduism is the second-largest religion in Sri Lanka, practiced by around 12.6% of the population. It was introduced to the island by Indian traders and settlers around the 5th century BCE. Sri Lankan Hinduism is heavily influenced by South Indian Hinduism, with a focus on the worship of deities such as Lord Shiva, Lord Vishnu, and Goddess Kali. The island has many ancient Hindu temples, such as the Nallur Kandaswamy Temple in Jaffna and the Koneswaram Temple in Trincomalee.
  • Islam is practiced by around 9.7% of the population in Sri Lanka. It was introduced to the country by Arab traders in the 7th century CE. The majority of Sri Lankan Muslims are Sunni Muslims, with a smaller population of Sufi Muslims. The country has many ancient mosques, such as the Jami Ul-Alfar Mosque in Colombo, which is also known as the Red Mosque due to its distinctive red and white stripes.
  • Christianity was introduced to Sri Lanka by Portuguese colonizers in the 16th century CE. Today, around 7.4% of the population is Christian, with the majority being Roman Catholic. Sri Lankan Christianity is heavily influenced by Catholicism and Protestantism, with many ancient churches, such as the St. Mary's Church in Negombo and the St. Sebastian's Church in Katuwapitiya.

Overall, Sri Lanka's religious diversity has contributed to the country's unique cultural heritage, with many ancient temples, mosques, and churches dotting the landscape. The country's citizens have also developed a strong sense of religious tolerance and respect for each other's beliefs, making Sri Lanka a model for religious harmony.

Traditional Attire

Image source: Unsplash

Sri Lanka has a rich cultural heritage and this is reflected in the traditional costumes and fashion of the country. The traditional costumes are varied and each region has its own unique style. One of the most iconic traditional costumes of Sri Lanka is the Kandyan saree. This is a type of saree worn by women in the central region of the country. It is made of handloom fabric and is decorated with intricate designs. The Kandyan saree is usually worn with a blouse and a shawl known as an uda davaniya.

Men in Sri Lanka traditionally wear a sarong known as a veshti. This is a long piece of cloth that is wrapped around the waist and legs. The veshti is usually worn with a shirt and a jacket known as a kabakurutuwa.

The traditional costumes of Sri Lanka also include a variety of headwear. Women often wear a floral headpiece known as a mandapaya or a jeweled headband known as a netti. Men traditionally wear a headwrap known as a cheetaya or a turban known as a pancha.

Overall, traditional costumes and fashion play an important role in Sri Lankan culture and are a reflection of the country's rich heritage.
